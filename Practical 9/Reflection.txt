This practical was a very helpful hands-on introduction to stochastic SIR models and their extensions. 
Overall, I found it challenging but rewarding, especially seeing how small changes in parameters or population structure can lead to very different outbreak trajectories.

What was easy / hard?
Setting up the basic SIR model in SIR.py was relatively straightforward. 
Defining the parameters, arrays, and the time loop made sense after writing the pseudocode. 
The trickiest part was correctly implementing the infection probability — remembering to multiply beta by the proportion of infected individuals took a moment to grasp.
The vaccination extension (SIR_vaccination.py) was easy to add once the base model worked. 
Looping over different vaccination percentages and plotting the results helped me clearly see the herd immunity threshold around 70–80% for the given parameters.
The 2D spatial model (spatial_SIR.py) was definitely the hardest part. 
Handling neighbour infections, especially at the boundaries of the grid, required careful thought. 
Using np.where() to find infected cells and looping over their neighbours was initially confusing, but the provided snippet helped me understand the logic. 
Getting recovery to work alongside infection without affecting the same time step’s newly infected individuals was a good lesson in state updates.

What I enjoyed most / least?
I really enjoyed visualising the disease spread in 2D — watching the infection ripple outwards from a single point and then slowly turn into recovery was fascinating. 
It made the concept of spatial structure very tangible.
I enjoyed the stochastic nature less when debugging, because running the same code twice gave different results, making it hard to tell if a change actually fixed a bug or just produced a different random outcome. 
However, I understand that this is exactly the point of probabilistic modelling.

Most important thing I learned
The most important takeaway for me was that simple probabilistic rules (beta, gamma) can produce complex and realistic epidemic dynamics. 
I also gained a much better intuition for herd immunity: even without vaccinating everyone, reducing the number of susceptible individuals below a threshold can stop the disease from spreading. 
In the 2D model, I learned how local interactions matter — a well-mixed assumption can overestimate spread compared to a spatial grid where infection only reaches neighbours.

Overall, this practical bridged theory and code nicely, and I now feel more confident implementing agent‑based or grid‑based epidemiological models in Python.
